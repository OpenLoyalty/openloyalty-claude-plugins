# Key Ingredients of a Winning Plan

Each ingredient below is essential. A complete winning plan addresses all 11. Evaluate which are strong, which need work, and which are missing entirely.

---

## 1. Buyer Committee Mapping

**What:** Identify every person involved in the buying decision — champions, influencers, decision-makers, blockers, and budget holders.

**Why it matters:** Deals stall or die when key stakeholders are unaware or unaligned. Mapping the committee early prevents surprises in later stages.

**How to execute:**
- List all known contacts at the prospect company and their roles
- Identify gaps — who is the economic buyer? Who is the technical evaluator?
- Assign relationship owners from the OL side (AE, C-level, marketing)
- Connect with each committee member on LinkedIn from AE + OL frontmen (C-level, Marketing, other AEs supporting the process)
- Track engagement level: cold / warm / champion

**Completeness check:** Every committee member has a name, role, engagement level, and an assigned OL relationship owner.

---

## 2. Gamified Loyalty USP

**What:** Articulate why gamified loyalty outperforms standard cart promotions and conventional loyalty programs.

**Why it matters:** This is OL's primary differentiation. The prospect must understand that gamified loyalty is a fundamentally different category, not an incremental improvement.

**How to execute:**
- Contrast gamified loyalty vs. standard cart promotions (discounts erode margin, gamification builds engagement)
- Contrast gamified loyalty vs. conventional points-based loyalty (points fatigue, low engagement, commoditised)
- Use concrete metrics: engagement rates, repeat purchase frequency, program ROI
- Tailor the narrative to the prospect's current state (no program → gamified, or basic program → upgrade)

**Completeness check:** The plan clearly explains the gamified loyalty advantage in terms the prospect's industry understands.

---

## 3. Build vs Buy USP

**What:** Make the case for buying a loyalty engine vs. building in-house.

**Why it matters:** Technical stakeholders often believe they can build loyalty themselves. This argument must preempt that objection with honest trade-off analysis.

**How to execute:**
- Frame in terms of time-to-value, total cost of ownership, and ongoing maintenance burden
- Highlight OL's domain expertise embedded in the engine (rules, tiers, gamification mechanics)
- Address the "we could build this" objection directly with specific complexity examples
- Reference the prospect's core business — loyalty is not their core competence

**Completeness check:** The plan includes a build-vs-buy argument tailored to the prospect's technical maturity and resources.

---

## 4. Loyalty Engine USP

**What:** Position Open Loyalty as a gamified loyalty engine, distinct from other solution categories.

**Why it matters:** The prospect is likely evaluating multiple types of solutions (full-service agencies, SaaS platforms, in-house). OL must be positioned in its own category.

**How to execute:**
- Map the competitive landscape: OL vs. full-service loyalty agencies vs. generic SaaS platforms vs. in-house
- Emphasise OL's engine positioning: API-first, composable, developer-friendly
- Show where OL excels vs. where competitors excel — be honest about trade-offs
- Relate to the prospect's architecture and integration requirements

**Completeness check:** The plan clearly positions OL against the prospect's specific competitive alternatives.

---

## 5. Gamified Loyalty Tactics

**What:** Show the specific link between promised KPI uplifts and the gamification mechanics that deliver them.

**Why it matters:** Abstract promises ("increase engagement") fail to convince. Concrete mechanics tied to measurable outcomes build credibility.

**How to execute:**
- Map each promised KPI uplift to a specific gamification tactic (e.g., challenges → repeat visits, progress bars → basket size)
- Explain the behavioral psychology behind each tactic (loss aversion, endowed progress, social proof)
- Reference similar deployments where these tactics delivered measurable results
- No abstract promises — every claim must link tactic → behavior change → KPI impact

**Completeness check:** Every promised KPI uplift has a named tactic, a behavioral mechanism, and a supporting reference.

---

## 6. Loyalty Program Look & Feel

**What:** Create a visual "wow" moment by showing what the prospect's loyalty program could look and feel like.

**Why it matters:** Decision-makers respond to visuals. A polished prototype or mockup creates emotional buy-in that slides alone cannot achieve.

**How to execute:**
- Create branded mockups using the prospect's logo, colors, and brand guidelines
- Show key user journeys: onboarding, earning, redeeming, tier progression, challenges
- Include mobile app screens, web portal views, or in-store touchpoints as relevant
- Make it feel real — the prospect should see their program, not a generic demo

**Completeness check:** Visual mockups exist that are branded to the prospect and cover at least 3 key user journeys.

---

## 7. KPI Uplift Projections

**What:** Quantify the expected KPI improvements from gamified loyalty vs. normal loyalty vs. no program.

**Why it matters:** The business case lives or dies on projected ROI. Three-way comparison (gamified vs. normal vs. none) makes the value of gamification explicit.

**How to execute:**
- Define the KPIs that matter to this prospect (repeat purchase rate, average order value, customer lifetime value, churn reduction)
- Model three scenarios: no program, basic loyalty, gamified loyalty
- Use industry benchmarks and OL case study data to support projections
- Be conservative — credible numbers beat impressive numbers

**Completeness check:** At least 3 KPIs are modeled across all three scenarios with sourced benchmarks.

---

## 8. Business Outcomes Forecast

**What:** Translate KPI uplifts into revenue and cost impact using the prospect's actual business data.

**Why it matters:** KPI percentages are abstract. Revenue impact in the prospect's currency and scale is what gets budget approval.

**How to execute:**
- Gather (or estimate) the prospect's key business metrics: revenue, customer base, average transaction value, purchase frequency
- Apply the KPI uplift projections to calculate incremental revenue, margin improvement, and program ROI
- Present three scenarios: no program, basic loyalty, gamified loyalty (with OL)
- Include a payback period and 3-year projection

**Completeness check:** A financial model exists using the prospect's data (or reasonable estimates) showing clear ROI.

---

## 9. Specific Case Studies

**What:** Present case studies from OL's portfolio that are maximally relevant to the prospect.

**Why it matters:** Social proof from a similar company, industry, or use case reduces perceived risk. Generic case studies are less persuasive than targeted ones.

**How to execute:**
- Select 2-3 case studies based on: same industry, similar scale, similar use case, or same region
- Highlight metrics: program adoption rate, engagement uplift, revenue impact
- Include quotes or testimonials if available
- Prepare to discuss implementation timeline, challenges overcome, and lessons learned

**Completeness check:** At least 2 case studies are selected with clear relevance to the prospect's situation.

---

## 10. Industry Expert / Loyalty Specialist

**What:** Identify and involve a loyalty program expert who has worked with the prospect's competitors or knows their industry deeply.

**Why it matters:** An expert who understands the competitive landscape adds credibility and can speak to industry-specific challenges and opportunities.

**How to execute:**
- Identify OL team members or advisors with relevant industry experience
- Prepare them with prospect-specific context before any introduction
- Plan their involvement: discovery call participation, workshop facilitation, or reference availability
- If no internal expert exists, consider partner network contacts with relevant expertise

**Completeness check:** A named expert is identified with a specific role in the sales process.

---

## 11. Strategic Partner Engagement

**What:** Engage partners who can increase the chances of winning — integration partners, consulting firms, or technology allies.

**Why it matters:** Some deals require capabilities beyond OL's core (e.g., mobile app development, wallet integration, in-store systems). The right partner fills gaps and may already have a relationship with the prospect.

**How to execute:**
- Identify what the deal needs beyond OL's core offering
- Map potential partners who fill those gaps AND have a relationship with the prospect
- Assess partner willingness and alignment (timeline, commercial terms)
- Define the partner's role: co-selling, implementation support, or technology integration
- Example: Loyyo for cart linking and Apple/Google Wallet integration; Intive for mobile app development when they already have a relationship with the prospect's brand

**Completeness check:** Any capability gaps are identified and a partner strategy exists for each.
